3. What is 21 \(\times\) 50 + \(\frac{8}{2}\)


This problem requires the application of PEMDAS (parentheses, exponents, multiplication/division, and then addition/subtraction). Thus, first you would solve 21\(\times\)50 and then solve \(\frac{8}{2}\). Finally you would add the two values.

\(21 \times 50 = 1050\)

\(\frac{8}{2} = 4\)

\(1050 + 4 = \textcolor{green}{1054}\)

4. Mr. Macafee is hungry. He notices a donut shop that is next to College Station High School, and he buys 53 dozen glazed donuts. How many donuts total did he buy if there are 12 donuts in a dozen?


Buying 53 sets of 12 donuts, Mr. Macafee essentially buys 53\(\times\)12 donuts.

\(53\times12 = \textcolor{green}{636}\)

5. Round 21031 to the nearest hundred.


The hundreds place is the third digit from the right. In this case, it is a 0. We look to the tens digit to see if we need to round down or up. In this case, the tens digit is a 3, so we round down to \(\textcolor{green}{21000}\).

6. What is the LCM of 18 and 32?


The LCM, or Least Common Multiple, is the smallest number that is evenly divided by both 18 and 32. Since we know this number is going to be evenly divided by 32, we check multiples of 32, and pick the first one that is evenly divided by 18. This happens to be 32 \(\times\) 9, or \(\textcolor{green}{288.}\)

Note: In math-talk, if one number divides another with no remainder, we call the first one a factor or divisor and the second number a multiple.

7. Mr. Tyler needs to write 45 questions for a test. On the first day, Mr. Tyler writes 1 question. On the second day, Mr. Tyler writes 2 questions. On the third day, Mr Tyler writes 3 questions. How many days will it take for Mr. Tyler to write his 45 question test?


This can be done two ways. The brute-force way is to write out a bunch of numbers starting from 1 and adding them to see when you reach 45 total. Using this method, we see that \(1+2+3+...+9 = 45\), so the answer is \(\textcolor{green}{9 \text{ days}}\)

That works pretty well, but what if we wanted to know how long before Mr. Tyler has 5000 questions? We can't sit and add all the numbers up to 100, that would take forever! Instead, we can observe that the sum of the first \(n\) numbers is \(\frac{n\times(n+1)}{2}\). For example, the sum of the first 5 numbers is \(\frac{5\times6}{2} = 15\), and the sum of the first 100 numbers is \(\frac{100\times101}{2} = 5050\). To see how long it takes Mr. Tyler to reach 45 questions, we see that the sum of the first \(\textcolor{green}{9}\) numbers is 45.

8. Which number is lower, \(\frac{7}{24}\) or \(\frac{11}{33}\)?


To compare fractions, we need them to have the same denominator, or bottom number. To get these fractions to have the same denominator is a bit tricky, though, because 24 and 33 are wacky numbers. Instead, we can turn \(\frac{11}{33}\) into \(\frac{1}{3}\). We also know that \(\frac{1}{3}\) is \(\frac{8}{24}\). Now, we can easily see that \(\textcolor{green}{\frac{7}{24}\text{ is smaller.}}\)

9. If Joseph has 3 shirts colored red, green, and blue, and 5 masks colored black, orange, purple, pink, and brown, how many different combinations of shirts and masks can he wear?


Since any shirt can be paired with any mask, we have 5 mask options for each of 3 shirts. This means that the total number of combinations is just \(\textcolor{green}{5\times3 = 15}\)

10. How many unique, symmetrical letters are in the word TYPEWRITER?


In the word TYPEWRITER, T, Y, E, W, and I are all symmetrical. However, we have to be sure to just count each letter once. This gives us \(\textcolor{green}{\text{5 unique, symmetrical letters.}}\)

12. What is 0 - 1 + 2 - 3 + 4 ... - 27 + 28?


From \(0-1+2… -27\) you can see that for each pair of two numbers subtracted you will get -1, and since there are 14 pairs that would give you -14. Then you would add the 28 and get \(\textcolor{green}{14}\).

13. What is the unit digit of \( \text{ }7^{12}\)?


When we multiply numbers, there is an interesting property that the unit digit of the answer is always the product of the unit digits of the two numbers we were multiplying. In other words, even though \(7^{12}\) is a huge number, our calculation is easy. Also, we see that the unit digits as we multiply them form a sequence (they repeat). We start at 7, go to 9, then to 3, then to 1, and back to 7. We can count through 12 numbers in this pattern and find that the unit digit of \(7^{12}\) is \(\textcolor{green}{1}\).

14. Edward lives 10 miles away from the gym. Since he doesn't have a gym membership, he walks his dog, Speedy, to the gym every day for his exercise. Edward walks at the rate of 4 miles per hour and Speedy runs at the rate of 10 miles per hour. As Edward walks, Speedy runs to the gym and back to Edward as many times as possible. What is the total distance that Speedy runs in the time that it takes for Edward to walk to the gym (in miles)?


Figuring out all the times and distances that speedy turns around at is quite hard. Luckily though, we only care about the total distance he travels. This means we don't even care about the direction changes! We therefore just find out how far speedy would travel if he were running in a straight line in the time it takes Edward to walk to the gym.

Edward will reach the gym in \(10 \div 4 = 2.5\) hours.

Speedy will travel \(\textcolor{green}{10 \times 2.5 = 25}\) miles.

15. Sarah has a class roster with 100 students. They must collectively decide on a class pet. Of the 100 students, there were 50 votes to get a snake and 90 votes to get a hamster. Assuming that everyone voted for either snake, hamster, or both, how many people voted for both?


If we simply add up 50 and 90, we get 140 people. That's more people than there are in the class! This is because we counted each person who voted for both a snake and a hamster twice. Since we know that there are 100 people in the class, we find out how many people we ``double-counted" by subtracting the class size from the sum of the votes for snake and hamster.

\(\textcolor{green}{140-100 = 40}\) people.

Alternate Explanation: Since we know that 10 did not vote for hamster, those 10 had to vote for only snake. Since we know there were only 10 from the snake group that voted only snake, the rest had to have voted hamster and snake.

16. The following circles are the same size, and each has a radius of 7. The centers of the circles are points D and B. What is the perimeter of the shape ABCD?

Hint: The radius of a circle is the distance from any point on the circle to the center.


Since a radius is the distance from the center of the circle to any point on its circumference, or edge, we can see that AB, BC, CD, and DA are all radii. This means that each has a length of 7 and we just add them all up to get the perimeter of shape ABCD.

\(7+7+7+7 = \textcolor{green}{28}\)

17. How many triangles exist in the following picture?

Hint: The best approach is to count the number of triangles of each size, and add those numbers up to get the total. There are 4 sizes of triangles.


We start with the smallest size of triangle. We count 16 of these triangles in the big triangle. The next size of triangle is the one made up of 4 of the smallest triangles. There are 7 of this size (don't forget the upside down one in the middle). The next size of triangle is the one made up of 9 of the small triangles. There are 3 of these triangles. Lastly, there is the big triangle itself.

\(16+7+3+1 =\textcolor{green}{27}\)

18. If a triangle with sides of length 6, 8, and 10 has an area of 24, what would be the area of a triangle with sides of 3, 4, and 5?


The formula for the area of a triangle is \(\frac{b\times h}{2}\) where b is the length of the base, and h is the height of the triangle. This shows us that if we half the base, the area gets halved. Similarly, if we half the height, the area gets halved. If we half both base and the height (as halving all the sides will do), we half the area twice. The area of the new triangle will be \(\frac{1}{4}\) of original area, or \(\textcolor{green}{6}\).

19. Rhinos have 3 horns and deer have 2. What is the smallest number of animals needed to have a total of 23 horns?


We are trying to get to 23 horns with as few animals as possible. Let's start by getting as many Rhinos as possible, as Rhinos have the most horns. We can get 7 rhinos before we reach 23 horns, and 7 rhinos gets us to 21 horns. We fill the remaining 2 horns needed with a deer, so we have 7 rhinos and 1 deer, or \(\textcolor{green}{\text{8 animals in total.}}\)

20. Masks come in packs of 3 and 4. What is the largest number of masks that cannot be obtained by combining some number of each package? For example, it is impossible to buy 5 masks, but it is possible to buy 7 masks.


We are going to start at 4, and try to find possible numbers of masks that are impossible to buy. But how do we know when we have found the highest number that is impossible? As it turns out, if we reach 3 number in a row that are possible, every single number beyond those will be possible. This is because if we have one number of masks that is possible to buy, that number plus any multiple of 3 will also be a valid number of masks, as we could simply buy masks in packs of 3 to cover the ``extra'' that we included.

We know that 5 is impossible. 6 is possible with 2 packs of 3 masks. 7 is possible with 1 of each pack, and 8 is possible with 2 packs of 4 masks. 6,7, and 8, are three in a row that is possible, so we know that \(\textcolor{green}{\text{5 is the highest impossible number}}\)

Fun fact: This type of problem inspired something called the Chicken McNugget Theorem, because mathematicians were trying to find out what the highest number of chicken nuggets they could not buy was.
